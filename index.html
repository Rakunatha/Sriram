02
Introduction

Hybrid Warfare &Cyber Terrorism

⚔️
Context
In hybrid warfare, cyber terrorism blurs the line between crime and armed conflict by targeting critical infrastructure to threaten sovereignty or strike terror.
⚖️
Legal Framework
Section 66F of the IT Act, 2000 (amended 2008) is India's key domestic law, criminalizing cyber-terrorism with life imprisonment as the maximum penalty.
🌐
The Gap
Section 66F operates outside International Humanitarian Law (IHL) principles that govern cyber operations during actual armed conflicts.
🎯
Aim
This paper analyzes Section 66F's effectiveness, alignments and gaps with IHL, and advocates for nuanced application respecting global humanitarian standards.
03

  • 02

  • 03
    Reform Proposals Propose reforms bridging peacetime and wartime approaches while respecting global humanitarian standards.

05
The Provision

Section 66F — Verbatim

Section 66F. Punishment for cyber terrorism.

(1) Whoever,—
(A) with intent to threaten the unity, integrity, security or sovereignty of India or to strike terror in the people or any section of the people by—
   (i) denying or cause the denial of access to any person authorised to access computer resource; or
   (ii) attempting to penetrate or access a computer resource without authorisation or exceeding authorised access; or
   (iii) introducing or causing to introduce any computer contaminant,
and by means of such conduct causes or is likely to cause death or injuries to persons or damage to or destruction of property or disrupts or knowing that it is likely to cause damage or disruption of supplies or services essential to the life of the community or adversely affect the critical information infrastructure specified under section 70; or

(B) knowingly or intentionally penetrates or accesses a computer resource without authorisation or exceeding authorised access, and by means of such conduct obtains access to restricted information, data or computer database... with reasons to believe that such information... may be used to cause injury to the interests of the sovereignty and integrity of India, the security of the State, friendly relations with foreign States, public order, decency or morality...

commits the offence of cyber terrorism.

(2) Whoever commits or conspires to commit cyber terrorism shall be punishable with imprisonment which may extend to imprisonment for life.

08
Comparative Analysis

Alignments &
Gaps with IHL

ALIGNMENTS ✓
  • Both protect critical infrastructure — 66F covers CII under Section 70; IHL protects civilian indispensable objects
  • 66F deters acts that could escalate to armed conflict (grid hacks, hospital ransomware)
  • Aligns with Tallinn Manual Rule 4: sovereignty violation via cyber means
≠
GAPS ✗
  • 66F is peacetime criminal law; IHL is wartime (jus in bello) — they operate in different legal regimes
  • IHL safeguards civilians strongly; 66F focuses on sovereignty/terror intent, overlooking wartime civilian harm
  • No mechanism to switch from 66F to IHL framework during escalation to armed conflict
09
Critical Analysis

Criticisms &
Challenges

  • 01
    Vague and Expansive Language Terms like "likely to cause" and "strike terror" are undefined, enabling over-broad application beyond genuine cyber-terrorism.
  • 02
    Lack of Designated Enforcement Agency No single specialized body mandated to investigate 66F offences, causing fragmented enforcement across NIA, CERT-In, state police.
  • 03
    Evidentiary Challenges Digital forensics in cross-border attacks is complex; attribution of state-sponsored cyber operations remains legally insufficient.
  • 04
    Risk of Misuse and Overreach Broad scope risks application to non-terrorist cyber activity — activists, researchers, whistleblowers may face disproportionate charges.
10
Reform — Draft Provision

Proposed Narrow Version

Section 66F (Hypothetical Narrow Version)

(1) Whoever—
(A) with intent to threaten the unity, integrity, security or sovereignty of India or to strike terror in the people by—
   (i) denying or causing the denial of access to any person authorised to access a computer resource; or
   (ii) attempting to penetrate or access a computer resource without authorisation; or
   (iii) introducing or causing to introduce any computer contaminant,
and by means of such conduct causes or is likely to cause death or injuries, damage or destruction of property, or disrupts essential services or adversely affects the CII under section 70; or

(B) knowingly penetrates a computer resource... and obtains access to information restricted for reasons of State security or foreign relations, with reason to believe it may be used to cause injury to the sovereignty, integrity or security of India...

commits the offence of cyber terrorism.

(2) Punishable with imprisonment which may extend to imprisonment for life.

KEY CHANGE: Removes "decency", "morality", "defamation", "contempt of court", "public order" from 66F(B) — restricting scope to genuine national security threats only.
11
Conclusion & Way Forward

Suggestions &
Recommendations

✂️
Narrow the Scope
Remove non-terrorism grounds (decency, morality, defamation, contempt, public order) to focus strictly on national security threats.
🏛️
Strengthen Enforcement
Specialized cyber-terrorism forensic units, mandatory digital evidence protocols, faster inter-agency coordination (CERT-In + NIA + NTRO + DCA).
🔒
Judicial Safeguards
Mandatory preliminary magisterial scrutiny before FIR registration under 66F; clear guidelines defining "strike terror" and "likely to cause" harm.
🛡️
Unified Cyber Policy
Embed 66F within PRAHAAR 2026, NCIIPC guidelines, and Joint Cyberspace Doctrine through real-time threat intelligence and public-private partnerships.
